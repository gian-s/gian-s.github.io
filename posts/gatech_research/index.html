Deep Learning for Autism Detection

Deep Learning models to detect when questions are being asked given an audio file for use in Autism detection
July 2021 · 2 min · Georgia Institute of Technology | Edit
Table of Contents
  • Summary
  • Role
  • Results

Summary#

During an Undergraduate Research Program, I worked alongside Professor David V. Anderson, and PhD mentor Desmond Caulley on developing deep learning models to assist speech pathologist accurately diagnose Autism Spectrum Disorder.

The motivations behind the work and the results I got from my experiments this summer can be found in this power point, which I presented during the final presentations at powerpoint portion of Georgia Tech’s Summer Undergraduate Research in Engineering/Sciences (SURE) program. My presentation and findings won me a 1st place prize amongst the other computing sciences researchers.

Role#

Based on previous research, speech pathologists know that 24 hours worth of audio recordings of a child, is sufficient enough data to make a reliable diagnoses for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). A system that can accurately detect certain metrics of these recordings would effectively save a lot of time for a speech pathologist that must listen to so many hours of audio recordings. My mentor and I wanted to see how effective deep learning models would be at detecting certain metrics such as number of questions asked. The model would, given an audio signal, could detect whether or not a question has been asked.

I was tasked with reading and implementing a paper with models that attempted to solve this same problem with the use of Recurrent Neural Networks. The audio data we would be working with would be noisy data, therefore we wanted to create baseline models we could compare our results to by using recordings of sentences that had been manually transcribed. The models I created were capable of handling text and audio data. The feature extraction for both data types were done separately and was something that my mentor and I had experimented with. I was able to replicate the models used in the paper and establish some baseline results that were comparable to those found in the paper. We later wanted to see how well newer Neural Network Architectures, such as Transformers would be able to handle text as well as audio features in our classification. I used transfer learning, with the help of some large Transformer models in order to fine tune the model to our set classification problem. The models did fairly well amongst text data, but did not yield great results in audio-only cases.
